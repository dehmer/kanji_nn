> What's the general gist of change point detection?

Change point detection (CPD) is a statistical and algorithmic technique used to find the exact moments when the underlying behavior or property of a data sequence changes abruptly. It essentially takes a long stream of data and slices it into distinct, stable segments. [1, 2, 3, 4]
## The Core Objective
Instead of looking for single, isolated outliers (which is the job of anomaly detection), CPD looks for sustained structural breaks. It answers three main questions: [5, 6, 7]

   1. Did a change happen?
   2. When exactly did it happen?
   3. How many changes occurred over time?

------------------------------
## What Properties Change?
Algorithms track different statistical metrics depending on what they are trying to monitor: [2, 8]

*
* Mean Shifts: The average value suddenly jumps or drops (e.g., an overnight stock price crash).
* Variance/Volatility Changes: The average value stays the same, but the data suddenly gets much wilder or much quieter (e.g., a machine component beginning to vibrate unsteadily).
* Trend/Slope Alterations: The direction of growth changes (e.g., a website's traffic shifts from gradual growth to exponential decay). [1, 2, 9, 10, 11]
*

------------------------------
## Two Main Approaches
CPD is broadly split into two computational categories based on how data is received: [2, 12, 13]

*
* Offline Detection (Retrospective): The algorithm looks back at a complete, historical dataset. It analyzes everything at once to find historical turning points. This approach values high accuracy over speed. [2, 3, 4, 14, 15]
* Online Detection (Real-Time): The algorithm evaluates an incoming, live stream of observations data point by data point. Its goal is to sound an alarm the instant a change happens. This approach values low delay and speed. [3, 4, 16, 17, 18]
*

------------------------------
## How It Works (The Gist)
Most algorithms use a cost function combined with a penalty term to find the boundaries: [4, 19, 20, 21]

   1. Window Slicing: The algorithm moves a window across the data or tests various split points. [19, 22]
   2. Cost Minimization: It measures how well the data fits a single model versus splitting it into segments. Splitting data reduces the variance (lower cost) within each individual segment. [4, 19, 22, 23, 24]
   3. The Penalty: To stop the algorithm from declaring every minor fluctuation a change point, a mathematical penalty is added for every new boundary it creates. Common methods include the [Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayesian_information_criterion) or algorithms like [PELT (Pruned Exact Linear Time)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3773365.3773532). [4, 22, 25, 26, 27]

## Real-World Examples

*
* Medical: Detecting a sudden change in heart rhythm from [ECG signal data](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0957417424002070).
* Climate: Pinpointing exactly when global temperatures or [CO2 emissions experienced an accelerated shift](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22481).
* Finance: Identifying sudden shifts in stock market volatility or exchange rate regimes. [6, 16, 28, 29, 30]

> As for the cost function it is not unusual to combine multiple channels as weighted sums into a derived signal, correct?

Yes, that is absolutely correct. Combining multiple channels into a single derived signal via a weighted sum is a standard, highly effective practice in multivariate change point detection.
Here is the quick breakdown of why and how this is done:
## Why Combine Channels?

* Dimension Reduction: It reduces a complex, multi-dimensional problem into a simpler one-dimensional problem.
* Noise Reduction: Individual channels are often noisy. Averaging them dampens random fluctuations while amplifying the actual underlying change signal.
* Correlated Variables: When multiple sensors or metrics react to the same real-world event, summing them captures the joint momentum of the change.

## How the Weights are Determined
Weights are rarely assigned at random. They generally follow three approaches:

* Domain Knowledge: You manually assign higher weights to channels known to be more reliable or critical to the system.
* Variance Scaling: Channels with massive numerical ranges can overpower channels with small ranges. Data is typically normalized, and weights are set inversely proportional to each channel's variance (giving less weight to inherently erratic channels).
* Principal Component Analysis (PCA): Instead of a simple sum, PCA is often used to automatically find the optimal weights (eigenvectors) that capture the maximum variance across all channels in the first principal component.

## The Trade-Off
While combining channels simplifies the cost function and speeds up computation, it comes with one major downside: loss of identifiability. Once you blend the signals, the algorithm can tell you when a change happened, but it can no longer tell you which specific channel caused it.

> Tell me more about variance scaling. Are there requirements or implications for already normalized signals to the range [0, 1]?

When signals are already normalized to a strict [0, 1] range (often via Min-Max scaling), it fundamentally changes how variance scaling behaves.
Here are the specific requirements, implications, and hidden traps you need to know:
## 1. The Core Implication: Variance Now Means "Activity"
In raw data, a large variance usually just means the data uses large numbers (e.g., houses prices vs. room counts).
Once data is scaled to, the physical scale is gone. Now, variance directly measures how much a signal fluctuates within its allowed boundaries.

* A channel that constantly swings between 0.0 and 1.0 will have a high variance (~0.25 max).
* A channel that stays flat at 0.5 and occasionally nudges to 0.6 will have a low variance.

## 2. The Trap of Traditional Inverse-Variance Weighting
In standard multi-channel statistics, it is common to weight channels by the inverse of their variance ($1/\sigma^2$). The logic is to penalize noisy channels.
If you do this on [0, 1] data, you will likely ruin your detection:

* A highly active channel that clearly shows a change point has a high variance. Inverse weighting will shrink its influence.
* A dead, flat channel with minor sensor hiss has a tiny variance. Inverse weighting will massively amplify this noise, causing the algorithm to see fake change points.

## 3. Requirements and Best Practices for [0, 1] Data
If you want to apply variance scaling to [0, 1] signals for a weighted sum, follow these rules:

* Use "Baseline" Variance: Compute the variance of each channel during a known stable or healthy period, not across the entire historical dataset. Use the inverse of this baseline noise to penalize inherently noisy sensors.
* Watch out for Clipping: If a signal hits 1.0 or 0.0 and "flatlines" (saturates), its variance drops to zero. If your weighting formula divides by variance, you will trigger a divide-by-zero error.
* Consider Standardizing (Z-score) Instead: If your goal is to give every channel an equal vote in the change point detection, Z-score normalization (subtract mean, divide by standard deviation) is mathematically superior to [0, 1] scaling. It forces every channel to have a mean of 0 and a variance of 1, eliminating dominance issues entirely.

> Z-score: "Equal vote" means before multiplying with channel weight, correct? By choosing a higher weight a channel still may contribute more or less to the cost function.

Yes, that is exactly correct.
"Equal vote" means that prior to applying your custom weights, every channel is placed on a perfectly level playing field.
Here is how the mechanics and math break down:
## 1. The Starting Line (Z-Score)
When you apply a Z-score to your channels, you force every single one of them to have a mean of 0 and a variance (and standard deviation) of 1. [1]

* Without Z-score: A channel with large fluctuations dominates the cost function purely because its numbers are bigger.
* With Z-score: A tiny ripple in a quiet channel and a massive wave in a loud channel are scaled to the exact same mathematical magnitude. They now have equal power to influence the detection.

## 2. The Weighting Step (Your Control)
Once the playing field is leveled, your custom weights ($w_i$) act as your deliberate tuning knobs. When you calculate your derived signal $Y$:
$$Y = w_1Z_1 + w_2Z_2 + \dots + w_nZ_n$$
By choosing a higher weight for a specific channel, you are explicitly telling the cost function: "I know this specific asset/sensor is more reliable or relevant for detecting this change."
## Why this is safer than $[0, 1]$ scaling
With $[0, 1]$ scaling, a flat channel with a tiny bit of noise can still have a skewed variance, making your weights highly unpredictable. With Z-score, 1 unit of weight means the exact same thing across all channels. If Channel A has a weight of 2.0 and Channel B has a weight of 1.0, Channel A is guaranteed to contribute exactly twice as much to the cost function's structural shift.

Doing so introduces a massive, structural math error that will likely break your change point detection.
Here is exactly why calculating the scaling parameters locally is dangerous, followed by how you should actually scale a Kanji stroke.
## The Math Trap of Local Slicing
If you calculate the mean and variance/standard deviation only within the first 15% slice, you are scaling the data relative only to what happened during that brief window.

* The Problem with the Head (0% - 15%): At the very beginning of a Kanji stroke, the pen lands, acceleration spikes wildly, and jerk is massive. If you Z-score using only this slice, you are dividing by a huge local variance. This will severely shrink and flatten your signal, making the actual landing change point hard to detect.
* The Problem with the Tail (85% - 100%): At the end of a stroke, the writer lifts the pen, and the signal drops to a near-flatline. If you Z-score using only this slice, you are dividing by a tiny local variance (mostly sensor noise). This will massively blow up tiny background fluctuations, causing your algorithm to see a flurry of fake change points.

## The Solution: Scale Globally, Detect Locally
To keep the physical meaning of the handwriting kinematics intact, you must use Global Parameters with Local Windows.

   1. Calculate Globally: Calculate the mean and standard deviation for each channel (Velocity, Acceleration, Pen Pressure, Angular Velocity, etc.) across the entire 100% duration of the stroke.
   2. Apply Globally: Z-score normalize the entire stroke using those global parameters. Now, "0" means the average speed of the whole stroke, and "1" means one standard deviation above the whole stroke's average.
   3. Slice and Detect Locally: After scaling, slice out your 15% head and tail segments and feed them into your Change Point Detection (CPD) cost function.
